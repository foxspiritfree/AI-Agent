# Experience Design Reference

Use this reference when a UI/UX task is about onboarding, repeated engagement, emotional memory, product moments, service experience, narrative, or making a flow feel worth continuing.

## Problem Type To Strategy

Choose the experience strategy from the felt problem:

| Felt Problem | Design Focus | Review Question |
|---|---|---|
| Too complex | Intuitive action | Can the user immediately know what to do next? |
| Too frustrating | Surprise or rhythm change | Is there a feedback moment that changes the emotional texture? |
| No achievement | Story, progress, or growth | Does the user feel they moved forward? |

Source cards: `D1`, `D1B`.

## Empathy And Story

- Create empathy by giving the user, customer, or protagonist a clear problem.
- Make the audience stand on the same side as the protagonist before asking them to care about details.
- Use a painful or annoying subject only when it helps the user feel the problem, not as decoration.
- Let users fill meaningful gaps; filling a gap can create a feeling of growth.

Source cards: `D1A`, `C3B1A1B1B`.

## Repetition And Growth

- Good repeated experiences make people know what to do, keep finding interest, and want to continue.
- Use difficulty choice plus feedback to create a sense of agency and growth.
- Leave space for users to act repeatedly and become better through action.

Source cards: `D1B`, `M1`, `DZ6A`.

## Tension And Continuity

- Unresolved questions can maintain attention through the Zeigarnik effect.
- Avoid gaps between important questions when designing a guided experience.
- Alternate dense/light information and active/passive moments to create rhythm.
- Do not create tension by hiding necessary instructions or outcomes.

Source cards: `C3B1A1B1`, `C3B1A1B1A`, `C3B5A`.

## Memory

- Experiences become long-term memory through emotion.
- Review whether the flow has a specific emotional moment: relief, confidence, achievement, surprise, recognition, or trust.

Source card: `Q1A`.
