# Decision And Onboarding

Use this reference when the task is about activation, signup, onboarding, pricing, checkout, choice architecture, participation, conversion, social proof, or perceived value.

## New User Experience

- New users are a high-attention group; use that attention to create a relevant experience task.
- Login or entry pages should connect to the user's goal, show service value, and then call for action.
- A new-user questionnaire can increase involvement when the questions connect the user to the product.
- New-user obstacles are often the largest obstacles, even when improving attraction feels harder.

Source cards: `A4A2A1`, `A4A2A1A`, `A4A3B`, `A2D5`.

## Beneficial Friction

Keep friction when it:

- Builds commitment
- Personalizes the experience
- Teaches the user what matters
- Creates a sense of progress
- Prevents a costly mistake

Remove friction when it only delays, confuses, or asks for trust before value is clear.

## Decision Interface

- Decision interfaces are shaped by concreteness, immediacy, and certainty.
- Improve perceived net value by making benefit and cost easier to compare.
- Customers are influenced by target value before entering the decision interface.

Source cards: `A4B2`, `A4B1`, `A2H2`.

## Choice Architecture

Use:

- Default option when the right path is low-risk and broadly useful.
- Mandatory choice only when the choice is simple enough.
- Active choice when consent, commitment, or preference clarity matters.
- Prompted choice when the user needs a timely nudge.

These techniques are weaker when the decision is simple and users already know their preference.

Source card: `AC6`.

## Progress And Continuation

- Pre-progress can make people feel a task has already started and increase continuation.
- Loyalty cards, milestones, and visible progress work best when the next step is clear and valuable.

Source card: `A4B`.

## Social Proof

- Social proof can promote action in a decision interface.
- Use social proof to reduce uncertainty, not to hide weak value.

Source card: `BK5`.

## Expectation Match

- Users make mistakes when design signals violate what they expect.
- Review whether colors, labels, affordances, or sequence imply a different action than the system expects.

Source cards: `A2D4A1A`, `AC14`.
